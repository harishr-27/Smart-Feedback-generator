The Great Depression: A Structural Analysis

The Great Depression of 1929 was not merely a stock market crash but a fundamental failure of the global economic banking system. While many points to Black Tuesday as the singular cause, the reality is more complex, involving agricultural overproduction and the lack of banking regulations.

Firstly, the roaring twenties saw a massive expansion of credit. Ordinary citizens were buying stocks on margin, meaning they only paid 10% of the value and borrowed the rest. When the market dipped, margin calls forced mass sell-offs, spiralling the market into freefall. 

Secondly, the agricultural sector was already in depression before 1929. Farmers had expanded production during WWI and were now facing a surplus, driving prices down. This meant a significant portion of the population had no purchasing power.

However, the response by the Federal Reserve was the critical error. Instead of expanding the money supply to provide liquidity, they contracted it. This caused thousands of banks to fail, wiping out the life savings of millions of Americans.

In conclusion, the Great Depression was caused by a combination of loose credit, agricultural weakness, and poor monetary policy. It serves as a stark warning about the need for financial regulation.
